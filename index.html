Healthcare Passport Mobile Application

Capstone Project - Iowa State University (2024-2025)

Full-Cycle UX Research & Design Study

Comprehensive research and design project addressing the critical challenge of managing personal and family health information across fragmented healthcare systems. The project employed a complete user-centered design process from initial problem framing through iterative refinement and formal usability evaluation.

Problem Statement: Users face significant difficulties maintaining complete, accurate, and easily accessible health records across multiple healthcare providers and systems, leading to communication difficulties during medical encounters, redundant paperwork, and potential delays or errors in care.

Research Methodology:

  • User Research: Conducted user interviews and contextual observations revealing that users employ a patchwork of methods including relying on memory, maintaining physical folders, and navigating multiple non-interoperable patient portals
  • Target Population: Identified "Busy Parent/Caregiver" as primary persona, managing health information for multiple individuals across different providers
  • Requirements Gathering: Synthesized user needs into core requirements for centralized storage, controlled information sharing, and reduced cognitive load

Design Solution:

  • Core Features: Profile management for dependents, categorized information storage (Insurance, Medical History, Allergies, Medications, Family History, Lab Results), and QR code-based selective sharing
  • Design Principles: Large, high-contrast fonts; clear purpose statements; simplified dosage instructions; progressive disclosure; consistent visual design system
  • Regulatory Compliance: Sleeve design maintains all legally required information (CFR Title 21) while prioritizing usability on outer layer

Evaluation Process:

  • Heuristic Evaluation: Identified 25+ usability issues in initial design, including weak information hierarchy, misleading labels, and uncertain feedback
  • Cognitive Walkthrough: Revealed confusion with list management, unclear interactive elements, and inconsistent navigation
  • Comparative Usability Study: Within-subjects design (n=8) comparing original vs. redesigned interface across four key tasks
  • Metrics: Task completion time, error rates, NASA-TLX cognitive workload scores, System Usability Scale (SUS) ratings

Results & Impact:

  • Redesigned interface showed statistically significant improvements in cognitive workload (p<0.05) for Tasks 3 and 4
  • Higher SUS scores across Tasks 2, 3, and 4 (p<0.05)
  • Particularly strong benefits for users with cognitive challenges: reduced errors, fewer rereads, increased confidence
  • Demonstrated that human-centered design addressing cognitive limitations can transform medication labeling from risk source to supportive tool

Prescription Label Redesign for Alzheimer's Patients

Human Factors Engineering Research (2024)

Accessibility-Focused Medication Label Design

Applied human factors engineering principles to redesign prescription medication labels specifically for patients with Alzheimer's disease and cognitive impairments. The project addressed the critical intersection of neurological impairment and patient safety through evidence-based design interventions.

Background & Context: Approximately 6.2 million Americans experience Alzheimer's-related cognitive deterioration, with medication administration representing a particularly vulnerable domain. Traditional prescription labels impose substantial cognitive load through small fonts, technical terminology, and complex phrasing, leading to increased risks of medication errors.

Research Approach:

  • Task Analysis: Conducted Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA) to map medication management workflow and identify failure points
  • Ethnographic Methods: Observed real-world medication sorting tasks with participants representing diverse cognitive abilities
  • Comparative Study: Evaluated traditional labels (12 potential error points) against redesigned labels (8 potential error points)

Design Interventions:

  • Visual Accessibility: Large black font on white background ensuring high contrast under varying lighting conditions
  • Simplified Language: Replaced "Take one tablet every 8 hours" with "1 Morning" or "1 Night"
  • Purpose Clarity: Clear labels like "Blood Pressure" replacing technical drug names
  • Regulatory Compliance: Sleeve format maintains all CFR Title 21 required information while prioritizing actionable details on outer layer

Evaluation Results:

  • Cognitively Impaired Participant: Traditional label: 4 errors, 2 rereads, 1 assistance request (score: -7). Redesigned label: 0 errors, 1 reread, 0 assistance (score: -1)
  • Non-Impaired Participant: Traditional label: 0 errors (but required cognitive effort). Redesigned label: 0 errors (minimal cognitive effort)
  • Results demonstrated 85% reduction in task difficulties for cognitively impaired users

Human Factors Principles Applied:

  • Cognitive load minimization through simplified language and visual hierarchy
  • Error tolerance through reduced complexity and clearer affordances
  • Consistency and standards through uniform layout and prominent information display
  • Recognition over recall by externalizing information that would burden working memory
